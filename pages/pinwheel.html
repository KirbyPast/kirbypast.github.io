M101,Pinwheel Galaxy

Pinwheel Galaxy

1.General Information

  • The Pinwheel Galaxy is a face-on spiral galaxy 21 million light-years (6.4 megaparsecs) away from Earth in the constellation Ursa Major.

  • It was discovered by Pierre Méchain in 1781 and communicated to Charles Messier in 1781. In 2006, NASA and the European Space Agency released a very detailed image of the Pinwheel Galaxy, which was the largest and most-detailed image of a galaxy by Hubble Space Telescope at the time. The image was composed of 51 individual exposures, plus some extra ground-based photos. 

    2.Discovery

  • Pierre Méchain, the discoverer of the galaxy, described it as a "nebula without star, very obscure and pretty large, 6' to 7' in diameter". William Herschel wrote in 1784 that the galaxy was one of several which had a mottled kind of nebulosity.

  • Lord Rosse observed the galaxy in his 72-inch-diameter Newtonian reflector during the second half of the 19th century and was the first to make extensive note of the spiral structure. To observe the spiral structure in modern instruments requires a fairly large instrument, very dark skies, and a low-power eyepiece. 

    3.Structure and composition

  • M101 is a large galaxy with a diameter of 170,000 light-years and around a trillion stars. It has a high population of H II regions, many of which are very large and bright. It is asymmetrical due to the tidal forces from interactions with its companion galaxies, which compress interstellar hydrogen gas and trigger strong star formation activity in its spiral arms.

  • In 2001, the X-ray source P98, located in M101, was identified as an ultra-luminous X-ray source, receiving the designation M101 ULX-1. Further observations showed that the system deviated from expected models, with the black hole being just 20 to 30 solar masses and consuming material at a higher rate than theory suggests. It is estimated that M101 has about 150 globular clusters, the same as the number of the Milky Way's globular clusters.
